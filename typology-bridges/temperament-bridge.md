# üß≠ The Four Classical Temperaments

Historically, temperaments described broad behavioural styles:

- **Sanguine** ‚Üí energetic, social, expressive  
- **Choleric** ‚Üí driven, assertive, goal-oriented  
- **Melancholic** ‚Üí reflective, detailed, structured  
- **Phlegmatic** ‚Üí calm, steady, harmonizing  

In DFM terms, these are not identities, but **clusters of axis tendencies**
expressed in certain contexts.

# üîÑ Temperaments and Wave Dynamics

Temperaments often describe **baseline wave patterns**, not fixed psychological
types.

### **Sanguine**
- broad, energetic expansion  
- flexible shifts between archetypes  
- occasional impulsive contraction  

### **Choleric**
- forceful, linear expansion  
- rigidity under resistance  
- difficulty accessing Value‚ÄìCare poles under stress  

### **Melancholic**
- slow, deep processing  
- contraction when overloaded  
- expansion through structure, clarity, and meaning  

### **Phlegmatic**
- stable center-of-gravity  
- low reactivity  
- contraction through withdrawal rather than intensity  

In DFM, these patterns reflect **how waves behave**, not who someone is.

# üß¨ How Temperament Relates to Archetype Access

Temperaments loosely describe which archetypes feel more ‚Äúbaseline‚Äù for an
individual ‚Äî not which archetypes they *are*.

- A **Choleric-like** pattern favors Driver/Builder/Architect modes  
- A **Melancholic-like** pattern favors Analyst/Sage/Healer modes  
- A **Phlegmatic-like** pattern favors Nurturer/Advocate/Healer modes  

But through development:
- all individuals gain access to more archetypes  
- rigid patterns become flexible  
- axis activation becomes more balanced  
- temperament tendencies soften and integrate
