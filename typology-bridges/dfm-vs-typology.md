# Dynamic Field Model vs. Traditional Typology Systems

This document clarifies how the Dynamic Field Model (DFM) compares to common
typology systems such as the MBTI, Enneagram, Big Five, and the classical Four
Temperaments.

It provides a high-level map of similarities, differences, and integration
points.  
The goal is to provide orientation ‚Äî not conversion, replacement, or typing.

---

# üß≠ Overview: What Typologies Do vs. What DFM Does

### **Typology Systems**  
Typologies describe *stable tendencies* in personality.  
They answer questions like:
- ‚ÄúHow does this person generally behave?‚Äù
- ‚ÄúWhat are their long-term preferences?‚Äù
- ‚ÄúWhich motivation drives them?‚Äù
- ‚ÄúWhat are their likely traits?‚Äù

### **Dynamic Field Model**  
DFM describes *moment-to-moment cognitive dynamics*.  
It answers questions like:
- ‚ÄúWhat cognitive mode is active right now?‚Äù
- ‚ÄúHow are different poles of thought interacting?‚Äù
- ‚ÄúWhat wave pattern is shaping behaviour?‚Äù
- ‚ÄúHow can coherence be restored or expanded?‚Äù

Typologies describe identity tendencies.  
DFM describes cognitive *movement*.

They are compatible, but not interchangeable.

# üîç Core Differences

Below are the fundamental distinctions.

---

## **1. Static vs. Dynamic**

| Typologies | DFM |
|-----------|-----|
| Provide stable categories | Provides dynamic states |
| Describe how someone *usually is* | Describes how someone *is right now* |
| Trait- or type-based | Movement- and axis-based |
| Tend to be identity-oriented | Avoid identity claims entirely |

---

## **2. Fixed Structures vs. Open Field**

| Typologies | DFM |
|-----------|-----|
| Use fixed lists of types (e.g., 16 types, 9 types) | Uses a continuous cognitive field |
| Types imply belonging to a category | DFM models temporary positions in a field |
| Types do not change moment-to-moment | Positions shift constantly with context |

---

## **3. Function/trait theories vs. Axis Tensions**

Typologies describe:
- functions  
- traits  
- motivations  
- preferences  

DFM describes:
- structural polarities (axes)  
- wave dynamics  
- archetype transitions  
- integrative intelligence  

DFM shows *why* certain tendencies appear in certain situations.

---

## **4. Description vs. Development**

Typologies:
- describe what is  

DFM:
- models what can develop  
- maps growth as expansion across the archetype wheel  
- frames flexibility as the main developmental metric  
- shows how contraction ‚Üí coherence ‚Üí integration occur over time  

Typologies freeze identity; DFM describes evolution.

---

## **5. Identity Risks vs. Cognitive Safety**

Typologies can lead to:
- over-identification  
- stereotyping  
- fixed labels  
- self-limiting narratives  

DFM intentionally avoids:
- assigning identity  
- predicting behaviour  
- categorizing people  
- diagnosing or evaluating personality  

It provides *interpretive clarity* without psychological risk.

# üß≠ Summary

DFM differs from typology systems in four key ways:

1. **Dynamic vs. static**  
2. **Field-based vs. category-based**  
3. **State-focused vs. trait-focused**  
4. **Developmental vs. descriptive**  

Typologies help people understand enduring patterns.  
DFM helps people understand how cognition moves, adapts, contracts, expands,
and integrates.

When used together carefully:
- typologies offer context  
- DFM offers real-time clarity  

But DFM stands on its own as a fully dynamic model independent of static
typing systems.
